Why Today? Edition No. 003  ·  July 1, 2026

Today's Shared Moment

This World Cup has already produced three penalty shootouts. It eliminated Germany last night. Tonight, America plays.

Why does everyone know the safest penalty kick — and why can't anyone take it?

Goalkeepers who stand still save twice as many penalties as goalkeepers who dive. Goalkeepers dive 97% of the time.

In a penalty kick, the goalkeeper must decide which direction to go before the ball is struck. There is not enough time to wait and react. Most dive left or right. Statistically, this is wrong.

An analysis of 286 penalty kicks from top leagues worldwide found that when goalkeepers stand still in the center of the goal, they save roughly one penalty in three. When they dive to either side, they save roughly one in seven. The difference is not marginal. Standing still is more than twice as effective as moving.

Where goalkeepers go — and what happens

Source: Bar-Eli et al. (2007), 286 penalties from top leagues worldwide. Goalkeepers stay in the center 3% of the time.

Both parties know this. The research has been cited in coaching programs and sports psychology courses for nearly two decades. Goalkeepers are not ignorant of the statistics. They dive anyway.

The reason was named by researchers at Ben-Gurion University: action bias. When a goalkeeper stands still and the penalty goes in, the failure feels worse — to the goalkeeper, the crowd, and the cameras — than when the goalkeeper dives and the penalty still goes in. An inaction that fails is experienced differently from an action that fails. The goalkeeper who dives and misses was at least trying. The goalkeeper who stands still and misses just stood there.

The same psychology operates in the other direction. The center of the goal — directly where the goalkeeper was standing before the kick — is also where the kicker has the highest probability of scoring. Most penalty takers avoid it. Aim for the corner and miss: at least you aimed for the corner. Aim for the center and the goalkeeper catches it: you walked up and kicked it straight at him. Researchers named this shame evasion. A missed penalty to the corner is regrettable. A missed penalty to the center is embarrassing. The distinction is not rational. It is social.

What this reveals is not a flaw in soccer. It is a precise description of how humans make decisions when they know they are being watched. We do not optimize for the outcome. We optimize for how the outcome will look if it goes wrong.

This is present in more decisions than penalty kicks. It lives in the interview answer you give because it sounds right rather than because it is true. In the treatment a doctor prescribes because it is expected rather than because the alternatives are better. In the strategy a manager recommends because it is defensible rather than correct. The center of the goal is available in most of these situations. It remains, in almost all of them, unshot.

Tonight, if it goes to penalties, watch where the goalkeeper goes. The center is right there.


Source: Michael Bar-Eli et al., "Action Bias Among Elite Soccer Goalkeepers: The Case of Penalty Kicks", Journal of Economic Psychology, 2007.
