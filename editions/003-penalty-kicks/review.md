# Edition Review — Edition 003

**Edition:** Why does everyone know the safest penalty kick — and why can't anyone take it?
**Date:** July 1, 2026

---

## What Changed During Research

**Meeting-stage surprising sentence:** Goalkeepers who stand still save twice as many penalties as goalkeepers who dive. Goalkeepers dive 97% of the time.

**Published surprising sentence:** Same — unusually stable. The Bar-Eli et al. (2007) data confirmed the meeting-stage candidate precisely.

**What changed:** The research added two layers the meeting hadn't named: action bias (the goalkeeper side) and shame evasion (the kicker side). The meeting identified the paradox; research gave it two names. The names made the edition about human behavior under observation, not just about penalty kicks.

**What research revealed:** The shame evasion finding — that kickers avoid the center not because they don't know it's safer but because a missed penalty to the corner is regrettable while a missed penalty to the center is embarrassing. The social distinction (regrettable vs. embarrassing) is the most transferable insight in the edition.

## What Almost Happened

**Fork 1 — Neuroscience of choking under pressure**
Research surfaced material about cortisol, working memory degradation, and the neuroscience of performance failure. The edition could have become a piece about why people choke. Rejected because: the paradox (knowing and not doing) is more interesting than the mechanism (why performance degrades). The mechanism belongs in a different edition. Future edition potential: HIGH.

**Fork 2 — Economics of penalty kick strategy**
Game theory literature on penalty kicks exists (equilibria, mixed strategies). The edition could have become a piece about strategic reasoning in zero-sum games. Rejected because: the game theory angle removes the human element (shame, observation, social consequences) that makes the story transferable. Future edition potential: MEDIUM.

**Fork 3 — Longer sports psychology survey**
The action bias finding extends beyond penalty kicks to other sports decisions (managers making substitutions, batters swinging at bad pitches). The edition could have been a broader survey of action bias in sport. Rejected because: the penalty kick is more specific, more counterintuitive, and more immediately relevant to tonight's game. The universal paragraph at the close does enough broadening work without needing more examples.

## What Felt Alive

The shame evasion finding. The distinction between "regrettable" and "embarrassing" is small enough to feel obvious once stated and large enough to explain the entire behavior. It generalizes immediately.

The close. "Tonight, if it goes to penalties, watch where the goalkeeper goes. The center is right there." — mischievous, actionable, specific. It earns the tone the topic wanted.

The surprising sentence. The 97% / twice as effective gap is exactly what the text message test requires.

---

## What Felt Manufactured

The universal paragraph ("This is present in more decisions than penalty kicks..."). It is true and it generalizes correctly. But it is constructed from the argument rather than discovered during research. It is the one section where the energy drops slightly. That said — unlike Edition 002's political economy section — it is short enough not to become a problem.

---

## What the Edition Taught Us

- **The publication does not have to feel serious.** Edition 003's emotional register is delight. It is still unmistakably *Why Today?*. The unifying quality is curiosity, not tone.
- **The anti-template protocol works.** Asking explicit structural questions before building prevented Edition 001's structure from being inherited by default.
- **The surprising sentence can sometimes be identified at the meeting stage.** This is the first edition where the meeting-stage sentence was confirmed rather than replaced by research. Unknown whether this is a consistent signal about this type of topic (behavioral paradoxes with established research literature) or a coincidence.
- **Different carry types produce different kinds of editions.** Editions that change physical behavior (003) have a different energy than ones that change memory (001) or attention (002). The reader's relationship to the surprising sentence is different when they're going to act on it tonight.

## Emotional Register

**Actual emotional register:** Quiet delight / mischief

**Carry type:** Physical behavior — the reader will watch the goalkeeper during tonight's penalty kicks differently

**Perceptual habit:** Ask whether the behavior you're watching is optimizing for success or for how failure will look. The person who dives isn't trying to save more penalties. They're trying to not be the goalkeeper who just stood there.
