## Object of Attention

**Name:** The center of the goal

**Description:** The zone directly in front of where the goalkeeper stands at the start of a penalty kick. Research shows this zone has the highest save rate (33%) of the three zones, because the goalkeeper stays positioned there when they do not dive. Goalkeepers stay in the center 3% of the time. The center is the optimal zone for both parties: the best place for the goalkeeper to save and statistically the highest-scoring zone for the kicker. Both parties avoid it.

**Portability:** The Object appears naturally in conversation as: "Did you know the center of the goal is the hardest to score from — and basically no goalkeeper defends it?" It reframes an action the reader watches routinely (penalty kicks) around a fact they've almost certainly never heard.

## Concepts

**Name:** action-bias

**Full statement:** When an inaction that fails is experienced as worse than an action that fails — regardless of the actual outcome probabilities — people will choose to act even when inaction is demonstrably more effective.

**First instantiated:** Edition 003

**Angle used:** Goalkeeper behavior in penalty kicks — diving when standing still is twice as effective

**Angles not yet explored:**
- Medical decision-making (prescribing active treatment when watchful waiting is indicated)
- Financial decisions (trading when holding is indicated)
- Management decisions (changing strategy when stability is indicated)
- Political decisions (intervening militarily when non-intervention has better outcomes)

---

**Name:** shame-evasion

**Full statement:** People optimize decisions not for the most favorable outcome but for the least embarrassing outcome if things go wrong. Regret (I tried and failed) is more socially acceptable than embarrassment (I did the obvious thing and it was wrong).

**First instantiated:** Edition 003

**Angle used:** Kicker behavior in penalty kicks — avoiding the center because "I aimed for the corner and missed" is regrettable, while "I aimed for the center and the goalkeeper caught it" is embarrassing

**Angles not yet explored:**
- Interview and hiring decisions (hiring the known quantity over the better unknown)
- Investment behavior (holding losing positions to avoid crystallizing a loss)
- Medical decisions (prescribing the established treatment over the better new one)

## Research Residue

- Bar-Eli et al. (2007) found the same action bias in other sports decisions but the goalkeeper/penalty case is the clearest because the optimal strategy is unusually well-documented.
- Kicker behavior follows a different pattern than goalkeeper behavior: kickers tend to aim for a corner even when they know the goalkeeper will dive toward that corner. Both parties are playing suboptimal strategies simultaneously for the same social reason.
- Post-match interviews with goalkeepers consistently describe the logic: "I had to go somewhere." The standing-still option is not psychologically available even when it is statistically correct.
