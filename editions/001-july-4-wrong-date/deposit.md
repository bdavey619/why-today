## Object of Attention

**The date printed on the Declaration of Independence**

Specifically: the date July 4, 1776 appears on the masthead of the document Jefferson drafted, not on the resolution Congress passed on July 2nd. The date traveled with the document, and the document was what most Americans saw.

This Object is concrete and highly portable. In conversation, it naturally becomes: "Did you know the date on the Declaration isn't actually when America became independent?"

**Potential future Concepts accessible through this Object:**
- The gap between legal documents and their popular meaning
- How administrative decisions become cultural facts
- Why the thing that gets written down often matters more than what actually happened

## Concept Deposited

**national-memory-is-constructed**

*Full statement:* National identities are built through accumulated commemoration, not through historical accuracy. Nations remember what they decided to celebrate, not what actually happened.

*First instantiated:* Edition 001
*Angle used:* Administrative accident → cultural truth (the date traveled with the printed document; no deliberate choice was made)

*Angles not yet explored:*
- Deliberate construction (when a government actively engineers a founding myth)
- Contested memory (when different communities commemorate different events)
- Memory revision (when a country changes what it commemorates and why)
- The failure case (founding myths that collapsed under scrutiny)

## Research Residue

*Things discovered during research that didn't fit this edition but may be valuable later.*

- The Liberty Bell was rung on July 8th, 1776 — when the Declaration was first publicly read. Not July 4th. The Bell's association with July 4th is itself a later construction.
- The phrase "Independence Day" was not used widely until the 1790s. Early celebrations were called "the glorious Fourth" or simply "the anniversary."
- Several signatories to the Declaration later expressed regret — not about independence, but about specific language in the document. The Declaration we commemorate is not quite the Declaration everyone agreed to.
- Adams was deeply conscious that he was living through history and tried to manage how it would be remembered. His letters show an unusual meta-awareness of historical narrative-building that might anchor a future edition.
