## Concept confirmed

**Colonial relationships don't end at independence. They continue as migration, language, and eventually as squad lists.**

France controlled Morocco for forty-four years. The migration that followed filled French cities with Moroccan workers whose children were born French. Those children's children are now playing in the World Cup — for Morocco. The colonial relationship didn't produce resentment expressed as protest; it produced diaspora expressed as football eligibility. The squad list is the long tail of the Treaty of Fez.

This pattern likely extends beyond Morocco and France. Any former colonial power with significant postwar migration from its territories will find similar dynamics in sport, culture, and identity. The World Cup makes it visible because FIFA's eligibility rules formalize it.

## Visual format that earned its place

**The 26-box origin grid.**

One box per player, colored by birth country. Morocco's 7 boxes (dark red) sit beside France's 6 (blue) and Spain's 6 (gold) — the former colonial powers contributing nearly twice what Morocco contributes. The visual makes the argument before the legend is read: Morocco's own national team is built from the people those other countries made.

Lesson: when the discovery is a distribution across categories, a grid of individual units — one per item — is more viscerally readable than a bar chart. The reader counts instinctively. The counting IS the discovery.

## Fork saved — France's internal tension

**What 2022 revealed about France.**

In 2022, when Morocco beat Spain and Portugal, France deployed 10,000 police nationwide. A man was killed in a hit-and-run in Montpellier after the celebrations. Forty far-right activists were arrested before they could reach the Champs-Élysées.

This is a different edition from 007. Edition 007 is about the squad list and the history behind it — a question about the past. The France-internal-tension question is present tense and sharper: *what is France actually afraid of when Morocco wins?* The answer involves immigration politics, the collapse of the black, blanc, beur ideal, and the specific geography of French cities where that tension is old and specific.

Strong candidate if Morocco advances past Thursday's quarterfinal. The timing would be right: a second consecutive deep run makes the question unavoidable.
