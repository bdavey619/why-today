# Questions

*The research agenda for Why Today? These are questions we genuinely do not know the answer to yet. Not a backlog of features. Not a wishlist. A list of things we need to discover in order to build the publication we're imagining.*

*Questions are answered by editions, postmortems, and deliberate experiments — not by discussion alone. When a question is answered sufficiently to act on, move it to the Answered section with the edition or experiment that resolved it.*

## Open Questions

### On the Selection Algorithm
*Primary research agenda for the next ten editions.*

**What makes a hidden question unforgettable rather than merely good?**
We can identify good hidden questions reliably. We cannot yet predict, in advance, which ones will produce the "I never thought about it that way" response consistently. This is the most important question in the publication's research agenda.

**Which categories of shared moments consistently yield strong editions?**
We have one edition. We suspect sports, holidays, and science produce strong questions — but we don't know this. After ten editions, we should be able to map which categories over-perform and which resist the editorial move.

**What is the relationship between the size of the shared moment and the strength of the hidden question?**
Does a world-historic event produce better questions than a medium-sized shared moment? Our initial hypothesis is that medium-sized events are sometimes better — they carry less ideological weight and are easier to step back from. This needs testing.

**Can the selection step be made more explicit?**
Right now, the choice between five finalist questions is largely intuition. What criteria, if made explicit, would consistently identify the strongest question? Are there tests that predict reader response before publishing?

**Is there a pattern in what makes a question "inevitable once you hear it"?**
The best hidden questions feel like they should have been obvious. They produce "of course — why have I never wondered about that?" rather than "interesting, I hadn't considered that." These are different responses. What produces the former?

### On the Surprising Sentence

**What makes someone repeat a sentence the next day?**
We believe every edition needs a surprising sentence the reader will carry into conversation. But we don't know what properties a sentence needs to be repeated — as opposed to appreciated and forgotten. Is it the information? The structure? The contrast with what the reader expected?

**Is one surprising sentence always enough, or do some topics warrant more?**
Edition 001 had effectively two — the Adams prediction and the Adams/Jefferson death coincidence. Were both necessary? Would the edition have worked with just one?

### On the Construction Algorithm
*Secondary research agenda — refining in parallel across the next fifty editions.*

### On Structure and Form

**What does a reader remember from an edition one week later?**
We have a theory (the surprising sentence) but no evidence. Knowing what actually persists in memory would reshape everything from the selection process to the editing process.

**How much explanation is enough?**
We know over-explanation is a failure mode. We do not know exactly where the line is. The carry test is our current heuristic — but it's a feeling, not a measurement. Can it be made more precise?

**What is the right length for an edition?**
Edition 001 is approximately 900 words of body text, plus visual elements. Is that too long? Too short? Does it depend on the topic? We don't know.

**Does the publication need a consistent voice across editions, or can each edition find its own?**
Great publications usually have a distinctive voice. But a publication whose topics range from tennis to national holidays to engineering may need different registers for different topics. Is voice consistent across editions, or does it live in the editorial move itself?

### On Publishing and Distribution

**Can this publication work daily?**
The editorial meeting for Edition 001 required significant time and genuine intellectual effort. At what frequency can we produce editions of this quality without degrading? This can only be answered by producing more editions and measuring what happens to quality over time.

**What topics consistently fail to produce strong editions?**
We have hypotheses (tragedies, live political crises, specialist topics) but no data. This will become clear through accumulated failure as much as through accumulated success.

**What makes someone share an edition?**
More specifically: is it the question, the surprising sentence, or the complete exploration that gets shared? The answer shapes both editorial and product decisions.

**Is there a format smaller than an edition?**
A surprising sentence and two paragraphs. Could that be its own format? An edition is the full museum exhibition. But is there a "postcard" format — a single observation that the reader carries without reading the full exploration?

**What is the right distribution channel?**
Newsletter, web, social, all three? These are not interchangeable. A newsletter rewards the edition's full exploration. A social post rewards the surprising sentence. A web page rewards archives and discovery. The answer affects how we optimize each edition.

---

### On Automation

**Can AI perform the rejection step reliably?**
The rejection step — choosing one question from five finalists — is currently the most human-dependent part of the process. It requires holding the questions against each other and feeling the difference. Can AI learn to do this? What training signal would it need? How many editions would it take?

**What is the irreducible human contribution to this process?**
We believe editorial taste must be learned before it can be automated. But we don't know which parts of that taste are teachable to AI and which are permanently human. This is the question that determines the long-term shape of the publication.

**How would we know when the algorithm is good enough to run without constant human supervision?**
We need a quality benchmark. What does "good enough" look like? What does a consistent, measurable signal of edition quality look like — beyond reading the edition and feeling whether it worked?
