# Failure Modes

*Named failure modes are easier to avoid. Every failure should become named. This document grows with every edition.*

---

## On Selecting the Question

### The Adjacent Question
**What it looks like:** The hidden question sits next to today's event rather than being earned by it.
**Example:** "Why fireworks?" for July 4th. The question is thematically related but the current event doesn't make it *more* interesting than it would be on any other day.
**The test:** Could this question appear in a general curiosity newsletter on any random day? If yes, the connection to today is not doing enough work.
**Fix:** Find the question that the current event makes newly and specifically interesting. Look for the question that today's event creates, not the question today's event reminds you of.

### The Wikipedia Question
**What it looks like:** The question is interesting but has a clean, satisfying answer available in three minutes of searching. No surprising sentence is possible.
**Example:** "Why do we shake hands?" or "What is the origin of the national anthem?"
**Why it fails:** The reader already knows they can find the answer. The curiosity gap closes before they invest.
**Fix:** The hidden question needs to reveal something that surprises even people who looked it up. If the Wikipedia answer is satisfying, the question is wrong.

### The Universal Without Anchor
**What it looks like:** The question is philosophically interesting but too broad to have a surprising answer.
**Example:** "How does a country choose its birthday?" vs. "Why is July 4th America's birthday if it isn't the date of anything decisive?"
**Why it fails:** Universal questions have interesting territory but no destination. They produce thoughtful essays, not surprising sentences.
**Fix:** Narrow the question until it has a verifiable, astonishing answer. The universal insight belongs in the exploration, not the question.

### The Headline Restatement
**What it looks like:** The hidden question is simply a better-written version of today's headline.
**Example:** Headline — "Paraguay upsets Germany." Hidden question — "How did Paraguay beat Germany?" This is not a hidden question; it is a game recap with different formatting.
**Fix:** The hidden question should feel surprising *to a reader who already knows the headline*. If someone who read the news could have asked the same question, it isn't hidden enough.

### The Partisan Trap
**What it looks like:** The hidden question touches a contested political issue such that even a perfect, balanced exploration risks being read as a political position.
**Example:** A question about voting rights that, regardless of content, will be read as pro-one-party by half the audience.
**Why it fails:** Trust is the publication's infrastructure. Once it is read as taking sides, half the potential audience stops listening.
**Fix:** Avoid live partisan fault lines. Historical political questions ("why did the Electoral College survive?") are usually safer than active ones ("why do mail-in ballots remain contested?").

### The Tragedy Pivot
**What it looks like:** A tragic event (a crash, a disaster, a death) is used as the entry point for a curiosity question.
**Example:** A skydiving crash → "Why do we voluntarily fall from airplanes?"
**Why it fails:** The pivot away from human suffering toward intellectual curiosity feels evasive, even when the question is genuinely interesting. The audience is not ready to be curious yet.
**Fix:** Do not use events with visible human victims as entry points for the editorial move. Wait until the event has settled into history.

## On the Hidden Question and Research

### The Missing Surprising Sentence
**What it looks like:** The question is good, the research is thorough, but no sentence emerges that stops the reader.
**Why it fails:** Without a surprising sentence, the edition may be informative and well-written but won't produce the "I never thought about it that way" response. It won't travel in conversation.
**Fix:** If serious research doesn't yield a surprising sentence, the question is probably wrong. Return to the finalist list. The surprising sentence is not a writing achievement — it is a research outcome.

### The Question That Doesn't Evolve
**What it looks like:** The edition answers the question exactly as it was stated in the editorial meeting, with no evolution from research.
**Why it fails:** Usually means the research was confirmation rather than discovery. The best editions find something in research that changes the shape of the question — making it richer, stranger, or more specific than it was at the start.
**Fix:** Treat the chosen question as a hypothesis. When research produces a more interesting version of the question, take it.

## On Writing and Structure

### Explanation After Understanding
**What it looks like:** The edition keeps explaining after the reader already grasped the point.
**Example:** Any section that begins "In other words..." or "To put it differently..." when the reader has already put it the same way.
**Why it fails:** It signals that the writer doesn't trust the reader. More than that, it costs the edition its confidence.
**Test:** At what point can the reader complete your next sentence before reading it? Everything after that point is over-explanation.
**Fix:** Apply the carry test. Once the reader can carry the surprising sentence into a conversation, look hard at everything that follows.

### The Interesting Fact Trap
**What it looks like:** The edition accumulates interesting facts without producing a changed perspective. The reader leaves knowing more but seeing the same.
**Why it fails:** This is an explainer, not an edition. Explainers are valuable. They are not what this publication does.
**Fix:** For every section, ask: does this change how the reader sees the thing, or does it add information to what they already see? The former is the mission. The latter is noise.

### The Thematic Summary Ending
**What it looks like:** The edition ends by restating the main idea. "So we see that nations remember what they decide to celebrate, not what actually happened."
**Why it fails:** The reader already understood the theme. Restating it makes the edition feel like an essay exam. It closes a question that should be left open.
**Fix:** Find the coda — something strange, specific, and resonant that ends the edition without summarizing it. The best codas feel discovered during research. Let the ending haunt rather than conclude.

### The Thesis Buried in the Middle
**What it looks like:** The surprising sentence appears in paragraph six of twelve.
**Why it fails:** Readers who don't make it to paragraph six miss the point entirely. Readers who do make it feel they waded through setup to reach the thing they came for.
**Fix:** The surprising sentence belongs near the top, in a visually distinct position. It is the thesis, not the climax.

### The Medium That Stayed Prose
**What it looks like:** Information that wants to be a timeline, map, or diagram is presented as a paragraph with "first... then... next..." structure.
**Why it fails:** Prose is inefficient for comparison, sequence, and spatial information. Asking prose to carry what a visual element would carry in seconds is a structural mistake.
**Fix:** When information involves comparison across time, geography, or scale, consider a non-prose element. Ask: what does this content want to become?

### The Beautiful Essay Without Frame
**What it looks like:** Strong writing, interesting ideas, no clear connection between the opening question and the closing revelation.
**Why it fails:** The reader enjoyed the ride but can't explain what changed. They cannot carry a surprising sentence into conversation because the edition didn't identify one.
**Fix:** Before writing, identify the surprising sentence and the revelation explicitly. If either is missing, the edition is not ready to be written.

## On the Edition's Identity

### The News Product Drift
**What it looks like:** The edition begins to explain today's events rather than use them as an entry point.
**Why it fails:** This is a different product with different competition. Explaining news is a saturated category. Using news to open timeless questions is not.
**Fix:** The edition should never tell the reader something they couldn't find in today's headlines. It should tell them something the headlines don't know to look for.

### The Topic That Belongs to the Reader
**What it looks like:** The hidden question is deeply interesting but belongs to a specialist audience rather than a general reader.
**Example:** "Why did Canada become the world's leading builder of space robotics?" is fascinating to space enthusiasts. It requires background most readers don't have and doesn't connect to their daily experience.
**Fix:** The hidden question should be surprising to a smart, curious person with no particular domain knowledge. If it requires domain fluency to appreciate, it is probably the wrong question.
