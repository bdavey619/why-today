# The Editorial Algorithm

*The scientific notebook of Why Today? Every meaningful discovery produces a new version. Versions are not improvements — they are refinements of understanding. The algorithm was always there. We are learning to see it more clearly.*

---

## The Two Algorithms

Why Today? runs on two distinct algorithms. They must be studied and refined independently.

**The Selection Algorithm** — Which question survives?
This is the harder problem. It requires comparing good ideas against each other and feeling a difference that is not yet fully articulable. The selection algorithm is currently the least explicit part of the process and the most dependent on human editorial judgment. Understanding it is the primary research agenda for the next ten editions.

**The Construction Algorithm** — Once a question survives, how does it become an unforgettable edition?
This is more tractable. Edition 001 produced several replicable patterns. The construction algorithm is being refined in parallel with the selection algorithm, but at a different rate. The next fifty editions will reveal its true shape.

*The software exists to express these algorithms. The algorithms are the company.*

## Current Algorithm: v1.0

---

### Selection Algorithm

**Goal:** Choose one question from among many good ones that will produce a surprising sentence.

#### Step 1 — Survey
Generate approximately 20 shared moments across diverse domains. For each:
- State the shared moment
- State why people are paying attention today

Do not editorialize yet. The survey is observational.

#### Step 2 — Generate
For each shared moment:
- Propose one hidden question
- Propose a candidate surprising sentence

*If a candidate surprising sentence cannot be proposed, the question is high-risk. Flag it. Questions without surprising sentences are usually wrong questions.*

#### Step 3 — Critique
For each hidden question, produce:
- Why it works
- Why it might fail
- Scores: Curiosity (1–10), Timeliness (1–10), Timelessness (1–10), Conversation (1–10)
- Binary: "Would someone bring this up later today?" Yes / No

Trust the binary more than the scores.

#### Step 4 — Reject to Five
Set scores aside. By editorial judgment, choose five survivors. For each:
- State why it survived (specific, not "it felt stronger")
- State what it beat and why

This step is where most editorial thinking currently happens. Document the reasoning carefully — it is the primary evidence for refining the selection algorithm.

#### Step 5 — Choose One
Hold the five finalists against each other. The strongest question typically has all of the following:
- Relevant to something the reader is about to *live*, not merely *witness*
- A surprising answer discoverable through research (not merely interesting territory)
- A candidate surprising sentence you would text someone
- A timeless idea visible through the specific answer

*v1.0 hypothesis, downgraded in v1.1:* The question relevant to a day the reader is about to inhabit may have structural advantages over questions about events they observe. Edition 002 weakened this: the heat dome question worked on its own terms regardless of timeliness. The timeliness was in the frame, not the question. Watch but do not rely on.

### Construction Algorithm

**Goal:** Transform a surviving question into an edition the reader cannot stop reading and will not stop thinking about.

#### Step 1 — Research
Find the surprising sentence. This is the non-negotiable deliverable of research.

*If serious research does not yield a surprising sentence, return to the finalist list. The question may be wrong.*

During research, also identify:
- The revelation: the philosophical payoff the surprising sentence opens up
- The coda: something strange and specific that closes without summarizing
- Medium-shift opportunities: what does this information want to become that isn't prose?

Allow the question to evolve. The surprising sentence you find may enrich, deepen, or change the shape of the question you chose.

#### Step 2 — Anchor
Write the surprising sentence before anything else. Everything else in the edition is built around it:
- Backward: what does the reader need to know to believe the surprising sentence?
- Forward: what does the surprising sentence open up toward the revelation?

#### Step 3 — Structure
Determine the edition's structure based on what the content wants to become.

*v1.0 default structure (depart from this when the content demands it):*
1. The Moment — one or two sentences orienting the reader in today
2. The Question — stated directly as the headline
3. The Surprising Sentence — displayed with visual distinction; the thesis
4. The Body — evidence organized by what the content wants to become
5. The Revelation — the philosophical payoff, stated confidently, with distinct visual treatment
6. The Coda — something specific and resonant; not a summary

*The structure is a default, not a template. Every topic should determine its own form.*

#### Step 4 — Write
Every paragraph must strengthen the frame. The frame is the question and the surprising sentence together.

If a paragraph is interesting but doesn't make the surprising sentence or revelation more meaningful, cut it — even if it was interesting to research.

#### Step 5 — Revise
Apply the carry test: at what point can the reader carry the surprising sentence into a conversation? Everything after that point must introduce new insight or emotional weight — not confirm what the reader already understood.

Check every paragraph against the frame. Check every section against the failure modes.

#### Step 6 — Close
End with the coda — something specific, not something wise. The haunting ending travels further than the wise one.

*Do not end by restating the theme. The reader already understood the theme. Find the strange, specific thing that makes the theme felt rather than stated.*

### v1.1 — Refined on Edition 002
**Date:** 2026-07-01
**Edition:** 002 — *What is the deadliest natural disaster in America?*

#### What changed
The living-vs-witnessing distinction has been downgraded from a stated selection criterion to a hypothesis under watch. See below.

The observation that Edition 001 became a quiet template by Edition 002 is now documented. See the framework-leading warning below.

#### What Edition 002 taught us

**Key evidence:**

- The surprising sentence identified in the editorial meeting ("heat kills more than hurricanes and floods combined") was replaced during research by a more specific and more shocking sentence ("a heart that fails at 3 a.m. in a 95-degree apartment is recorded as heart failure"). This happened in Edition 001 also. The editorial meeting selects the question; research discovers the true surprising sentence. These are now confirmed as distinct steps.

- Research again changed the edition's angle. The overnight low mechanism was not identified during the editorial meeting and became the edition's primary structural insight. The coda (Klinenberg/social isolation) was entirely unknown at the time of question selection.

- The Discovered Coda appeared for the second time. The Klinenberg finding was found during research, recognized as a coda, and placed at the close. It had the same quality as Edition 001's Adams/Jefferson coincidence: specific, strange, and not composable from the edition's argument.

- Edition 001 quietly became a template. The Revelation Block, the Coda slot, the Reading List, and the Section Labels all appeared in Edition 002 because Edition 001 had them — not because Edition 002 demanded each element independently. This was noticed during post-edition reflection but not during production. The framework led in ways that were invisible in the moment.

- The weakest section in Edition 002 was the one most built rather than found. The political economy paragraph ("if hurricanes had a tenfold undercount, there would be congressional hearings") is an argumentative move constructed from the revelation rather than a discovery made during research. It may be a consistent signal: manufactured sections feel different from discovered ones, and the difference is detectable in retrospect.

#### What questions remain unresolved

- Is every edition's true surprising sentence discovered during research rather than identified during the meeting? Or was this a coincidence in both editions?
- Does the publication make arguments, or does it make discoveries and stop? Edition 002's political economy section was an argument. The rest was discovery. The argument felt weaker in retrospect.
- Can the template trap be avoided through deliberate structural questioning, or does it require an explicit protocol?

## What We Are Watching

The following observations are being tracked across editions. Confidence levels assigned using the publication's taxonomy: **Observation** (witnessed once) → **Hypothesis** (seems likely but unconfirmed) → **Candidate Pattern** (recurred without being forced, named in PATTERNS.md) → **Confirmed Pattern** (three or more instances) → **Principle** (relied upon).

- **The discovered coda.** *[Hypothesis — 2 instances]* Both Edition 001 and Edition 002 codas were found during research, not composed during writing. The pattern is appearing consistently. If it appears in Edition 003, promote to Candidate Pattern.

- **Research changes the surprising sentence.** *[Hypothesis — 2 instances]* In both editions, the surprising sentence identified in the editorial meeting was replaced during research by a more specific, more shocking sentence. The editorial meeting may be selecting the *question* more than the *sentence*. If this recurs in Edition 003, it should change how we describe what the editorial meeting is for.

- **Question evolution through research.** *[Hypothesis — 2 instances]* The question reliably improves between selection and publication because research reveals a richer angle. Related to the above but distinct: the question's *shape* changes, not just the sentence.

- **The living-vs-witnessing distinction in question selection.** *[Observation — weakening]* Originally stated as a structural advantage for questions about days readers inhabit vs. events they observe. Edition 002 weakens this: the heat dome question would have worked on any summer day, not specifically July 1, 2026. The timeliness frame ("tonight, it will not cool down") was added during writing, not during selection. Demoted from selection criterion to observation under watch.

- **The foreknowledge advantage.** *[Observation — 1 instance]* Revealing the surprising sentence before showing the evidence may change how readers experience the evidence — making it investigative rather than informational. Not tested in Edition 002, which used a different structure.

- **The framework-leading trap.** *[Observation — 1 instance]* By Edition 002, Edition 001's structure had become a quiet template. The Revelation Block, Coda slot, Reading List, and Section Labels appeared without being questioned. The publication's structure should be rederived from each edition's content, not inherited from the previous one. Unknown whether this is avoidable or whether it requires an active countermeasure.

- **Manufactured sections feel different from discovered sections.** *[Observation — 1 instance]* The weakest section of Edition 002 was built from the revelation's argument rather than found during research. The energy differential was detectable in retrospect. Unknown whether this is a reliable signal or a post-hoc rationalization.
