# Editorial Playbook

*The practical handbook for producing a Why Today? edition. Written for someone joining the team two years from now. Philosophy belongs in EDITORIAL_PRINCIPLES.md and HEURISTICS.md. This document is practice.*

---

## Editorial Objective

Why Today? uses a current shared moment as an entry point into a deeper structure, history, place, or way of life. The shared moment is the door. The edition is what's behind it.

The edition succeeds when it creates a durable lens the reader carries beyond the page — when they will notice something differently tomorrow that they were already walking past today. Facts, research, visuals, quotes, and narrative exist in service of that perceptual change. Knowledge is not the end product. Curiosity is.

**An edition should create a durable lens, not merely deliver durable knowledge.**

A reader should finish the edition wanting to keep looking.

---

## Overview

Every edition moves through five phases:

1. **Editorial Meeting** — identify today's shared moments and find the hidden question
2. **Research** — find the surprising sentence and let the question evolve
3. **Writing** — build the edition around the surprising sentence
4. **Revision** — cut everything that doesn't strengthen the frame
5. **Final Review** — verify against FAILURE_MODES.md and publish

The edition is finished when the reader can carry the surprising sentence into a conversation. Not before.

---

## Phase 1 — Editorial Meeting

**Time budget:** 60–90 minutes each morning.

**Goal:** Choose one hidden question from among many good ones.

### Step 1A — Survey Today's World

Search broadly. Do not limit yourself to major headlines.

Scan across: international news, sports, technology, science, business, culture, weather, travel, architecture, design, history, local stories with broader significance, recurring annual events, anniversaries, anything that feels culturally present.

Produce approximately 20 shared moments. For each, write:
- The shared moment (one phrase)
- One sentence: why people are paying attention today

**Important:** You are looking for the conversations people are already having, not the most important events. A medium story that touches something universal beats a major story that stays in its lane.

### Step 1B — Generate Hidden Questions

For every shared moment, propose one hidden question.

The hidden question should:
- Be naturally connected to today's event (not merely adjacent to it)
- Create immediate curiosity
- Lead toward timeless ideas
- Have a surprising answer (not just a surprising direction)
- Change how someone experiences today's event
- Make someone say "I never thought to ask that"

For each hidden question, also propose a **candidate surprising sentence** — one sentence that, if true, would make the reader stop. If you cannot identify a candidate surprising sentence at this stage, flag the question as high-risk. Questions without surprising sentences are usually wrong questions.

### Step 1C — Self-Critique

For every hidden question, produce:
- Why this works
- Why this might fail
- Curiosity score (1–10): does the question make you immediately want to know the answer?
- Timeliness score (1–10): how tightly does today's event create the entry point?
- Timelessness score (1–10): how durable is the question beyond today?
- Conversation score (1–10): would the reader bring this up later today?
- **Bring up later? Yes / No** — trust this gut check more than the scores

### Step 1D — Choose the Best Five

Set the scores aside.

Choose five surviving questions by editorial judgment. For each, articulate why it survived while others didn't. Be specific. "It's more interesting" is not a reason.

The comparison is where the real thinking happens. Hold the five finalists against each other. Look for the question that:
- Is relevant to something the reader is about to *live*, not just *witness*
- Has the most surprising answer
- Produces the sentence you'd most want to text someone

### Step 1E — Choose One

One question. One edition.

Document the rejected finalists and why they lost. This is not bookkeeping — the rejected questions often reveal what makes the chosen question strong by contrast. (See REVIEW_TEMPLATE.md.)

## Phase 2 — Research

**Goal:** Find the surprising sentence. Let the question evolve.

The question you chose in Phase 1 is a hypothesis. Research often changes it.

### What to look for:

- **The surprising sentence**: one fact, coincidence, paradox, or reversal that makes an informed reader stop. If you cannot find it after serious research, the question may be wrong. Return to the finalist list.
- **The visual structure**: what does this content want to *become*? If the information involves comparison across time, consider a timeline. If it involves geography, consider a map. If it involves scale, consider a diagram. The medium-shift should serve understanding, not aesthetics.
- **The revelation**: what is the philosophical payoff — the thing the surprising sentence opens up? This becomes the emotional center of the edition.
- **The coda**: something strange, specific, and resonant that closes the edition without summarizing it. The best codas feel discovered, not constructed.

### Research principle:

*The surprising sentence you find may change the shape of the question.* Edition 001 was planned around "why is July 4th the wrong date?" but the research revealed the Adams/Jefferson death coincidence, which retroactively enriched the question and provided the coda. Let the research teach you what the edition is actually about.

---

## Phase 3 — Writing

**Goal:** Build the edition around the surprising sentence.

### Structure (not a template — a default to depart from):

1. **The Moment** — one or two sentences grounding the reader in today. They should already know this is happening. You are orienting, not informing.
2. **The Question** — stated directly, confidently. This is the headline.
3. **The Surprising Sentence** — displayed with visual distinction. This is the thesis.
4. **The Body** — evidence, context, and exploration. The structure should follow what the content wants to become.
5. **The Revelation** — the philosophical payoff. One idea, stated with confidence. Can receive distinct visual treatment.
6. **The Coda** — something strange and specific. Not a summary. An afterthought that echoes.

### Writing principles:

- **Write the surprising sentence first**, then build what the reader needs to believe it (backward) and what it opens up (forward).
- **Every paragraph must strengthen the frame.** If a paragraph is interesting but doesn't make the surprising sentence or the revelation more meaningful, cut it.
- **The essay is evidence that the question is worth asking**, not an exhaustive answer to it. Leave the reader with more curiosity than they arrived with.
- **Stop before the reader is ahead of you.** The moment the reader can complete your sentence, your sentence is unnecessary.

---

## Phase 4 — Revision

**Goal:** Remove everything that doesn't earn its place.

### The carry test:
Read the full draft. At what point can the reader now carry the surprising sentence into a conversation? Everything after that point must add new insight or emotional weight — not confirm what the reader already understood.

### Revision checklist:

- [ ] Can the surprising sentence be identified in under 10 seconds of skimming?
- [ ] Does every section introduce something the reader didn't have from the previous section?
- [ ] Is there a visual structure (timeline, map, diagram, blockquote) that reduces the weight prose has to carry?
- [ ] Does the revelation feel earned, or does it feel declared?
- [ ] Does the coda end with something specific, or does it end with a theme?
- [ ] Is anything explained twice? (If yes, cut the first instance.)
- [ ] Is there anything that was interesting to research but doesn't strengthen the frame?

---

## Phase 5 — Final Review

**Goal:** Verify against known failure modes before publishing.

Run the draft against FAILURE_MODES.md. Every named failure mode should be checked explicitly.

### Publication checklist:

- [ ] The shared moment is grounded in today
- [ ] The hidden question creates genuine curiosity (not just interest)
- [ ] The surprising sentence is specific, verifiable, and stops a reader
- [ ] The surprising sentence appears prominently — visually distinct
- [ ] The body earns the revelation
- [ ] The revelation is stated confidently, not hedged
- [ ] The coda is specific and resonant, not thematic
- [ ] No section over-explains what the reader already understood
- [ ] At least one non-prose element (if the content warrants it)
- [ ] Edition has been checked against FAILURE_MODES.md
- [ ] **Tomorrow, what ordinary thing will the reader notice differently because of this edition?** (Name it concretely — a billboard, a weather forecast, a squad roster, a historical date. If there is no clear answer, the edition may be informative rather than transformational.)

### Postmortem:

Complete the REVIEW_TEMPLATE.md immediately after publishing. While the editorial decisions are fresh. The review is not optional — it is how the publication learns.

## Notes on Workflow

**On AI assistance:**
AI is most useful for: generating candidate moments and hidden questions, researching the surprising sentence, drafting body sections, identifying visual structures. AI is least reliable for: evaluating which question is strongest, knowing when to stop explaining, finding the coda. The editorial judgment steps require a human in the loop until the selection algorithm is better understood.

**On publishing frequency:**
Do not publish a weak edition because a day passed. A skipped day is better than a wrong question. Quality sets the cadence; the calendar does not.

**On the editorial meeting record:**
Every editorial meeting should be documented. The rejected questions, the scoring, the reasoning — all of it. These records are how we discover the selection algorithm. See QUESTIONS.md for what we're still trying to learn.
