# Heuristics

*The accumulated editorial wisdom of Why Today? Every heuristic here was earned by editorial experience, not assumed in advance. When a new heuristic is discovered, add it with the edition or conversation that produced it.*

**Confidence levels:** Each heuristic carries an implicit confidence. When confidence is uncertain or a heuristic is unconfirmed, it is tagged. Untagged heuristics have appeared consistently in both editions. Tagged heuristics require more evidence before being relied upon.

- `[Observation]` — noticed in one edition; may not generalize
- `[Hypothesis]` — seems likely but requires more evidence to trust
- `[Confirmed]` — relied upon across multiple editions without contradiction

## On Selecting the Shared Moment

**The best shared moments are days the reader is about to live, not merely witness.** `[Hypothesis]`
A sporting event is witnessed. A national holiday is lived. An election result is witnessed. A heat wave is lived. The closer the shared moment is to something the reader will inhabit — not just observe — the more the hidden question can change their actual experience.
*(Source: Edition 001 postmortem)*
*Confidence downgraded after Edition 002: the heat dome question's strength came from the overnight low mechanism and death certificate insight — both timeless. The timeliness frame ("tonight, it will not cool down") was added during writing, not during selection. The question would have worked on any hot summer day. Watch but do not rely on.*

**A medium story that touches something universal beats a major story that stays in its lane.**
A World Cup upset is a major story. An upset that reveals something about human performance under pressure is universal. The size of the event is less important than the depth of the question it contains.

**Tragedies and live crises resist the editorial move.**
The hidden question move (event → timeless question → changed perspective) requires enough distance from the event to make the pivot feel earned rather than evasive. Active tragedies, breaking crises, and events with visible human suffering usually cannot support this move. Know what kinds of events the publication will not engage with.

## On Finding the Hidden Question

**The question is more important than the essay.**
If someone would text a friend the question alone — without reading the essay — the question is right. The essay is evidence that the question is worth asking. See also: the text message test.

**Enter specific. Exit universal.**
"Why is July 4th America's birthday?" is stronger than "How does a country choose its birthday?" — not because it's more interesting, but because it has a surprising *answer*. Specific questions create surprising sentences. Universal questions create interesting territory. Start specific; let the exploration widen.
*(Source: Edition 001 editorial meeting)*

**The hidden question must be earned by the event, not merely adjacent to it.**
A weak hidden question sits next to today's event. A strong hidden question is one that the event makes newly and compellingly interesting — where you couldn't have asked it as urgently without today's context.
*Test: Could this question appear in a general curiosity newsletter on any random day? If yes, the connection to today is not doing enough work.*

**If you cannot find a candidate surprising sentence, the question is probably wrong.**
Before committing to a hidden question, try to identify the surprising sentence it would generate. If you can't find one — if the question is interesting but produces no verifiable, astonishing fact or reversal — treat this as a signal to return to the finalist list.
*(Source: EDITORIAL_PLAYBOOK.md, Phase 2)*

**Questions evolve through research.**
The question you select in the editorial meeting is a hypothesis. Research often changes its shape. The surprising sentence you find may retroactively enrich the question. Let it. The edition should be about the best version of the question, not the first version.
*(Source: Edition 001 postmortem — Adams/Jefferson coincidence changed the coda)*

**The rejection process is where editorial thinking happens.**
Generating 20 candidates is generative and largely mechanical. Choosing one from five finalists is genuinely editorial. The comparison reveals what makes the chosen question strong in ways that evaluating it in isolation cannot. Never skip the rejection step.
*(Source: Edition 001 postmortem)*

## On the Surprising Sentence

**Every edition needs one surprising sentence.**
Not a well-written sentence. A sentence whose *content* — not its writing — is surprising enough to stop a reader. This sentence is the thesis. Everything before it builds toward it. Everything after it explores its implications.

**The surprising sentence should be identifiable before writing begins.**
It is the destination. Without knowing it in advance, you are more likely to bury it in the middle or miss it entirely. If you cannot find it before writing, return to research.

**The surprising sentence is what the reader will carry into conversation.**
The edition's social value is carried entirely by this sentence. If the reader cannot repeat it tomorrow, the edition did not fully work. Design the edition to make this sentence unforgettable.

**The text message test.**
Would someone text a friend the surprising sentence alone — without the essay? If yes, the edition is working. If not, the sentence is not surprising enough, or the question is not right.

## On Structure and Form

**If the object wants to become a timeline instead of prose, let it.**
Every edition should ask: what does this content want to *become*? Information organized along time wants to be a timeline. Information organized across geography wants to be a map. Information about scale wants to be a diagram. The medium-shift should serve understanding, not aesthetics.
*(Source: Edition 001 postmortem)*

**The edition earns one moment of visual rupture.**
One point where the visual register changes — a dark block, a full-bleed image, an isolated pull quote — creates rhythm and marks the emotional center. More than one rupture and the emphasis is lost. Use it for the revelation.

**Every paragraph must strengthen the frame.**
The frame is the question and the surprising sentence together. If a paragraph is interesting but doesn't make the surprising sentence or the revelation more meaningful, it weakens the edition by diluting attention. Cut it, even if it was interesting to research.

## On Writing and Revision

**Stop explaining as soon as the reader understands.**
More precisely: stop once the reader can carry the surprising sentence into a conversation. Every word after that point is a cost — it dilutes the edition's confidence and risks leaving the reader ahead of the writer.
*(Source: Brett's observation, Edition 001 feedback)*

**The essay is evidence, not the argument.**
The question creates the curiosity. The surprising sentence creates the "I never thought about that" moment. The essay provides the evidence that makes both credible. An essay that tries to be the argument usually over-explains.

**The coda is emotional punctuation, not thematic summary.**
Do not end by restating the theme. Find something strange, specific, and resonant that closes the edition without closing the question. The best codas feel discovered during research, not constructed during writing.
*(Source: Edition 001 — Adams/Jefferson dying on July 4th, 1826)*

**End with something specific, not something wise.**
Wise endings ("nations remember what they decide to celebrate") feel complete. Specific endings ("Thomas Jefferson survives." He didn't.) feel haunting. The haunting ending travels further.

## On Editorial Judgment

**The "I never thought about it that way" test.**
This is the north star. Not: did the reader learn something? Not: did the reader enjoy it? Did the reader experience a small, permanent shift in how they see something they already knew?

**The dinner conversation test.**
Would the reader bring something from this edition into a conversation tonight? If no, the edition failed its primary social purpose.

**Publish less rather than worse.**
A skipped day is better than a weak edition. Publishing a weak edition establishes the wrong expectation. Quality sets the publishing cadence; the calendar does not.

## On the Publication's Identity

**We are not explaining today's news. We are making today's conversations more interesting.**
The reader brings their own awareness of the event. We bring a different lens. The edition should leave the reader better equipped for today's conversations, not better informed about today's events.

**The edition's deepest work is perspective, not information.**
When the subject is another culture, economy, or way of life, the edition succeeds not when the reader learns a fact but when they understand how other people experience their daily lives differently. The "I never thought about it that way" test has a deeper form: "I never understood how *they* see it." An edition that achieves this leaves the reader with a permanent lens — a way of reading everything they will encounter about that place or system afterward.
*(Source: Edition 009 editorial conversation — SK Hynix / chaebol economy)*

**The question is the product. The essay is the evidence.**
The intellectual property of this publication is not its writing. It is its ability to repeatedly find the questions that create genuine curiosity. Protect and develop that capacity above everything else.
*(Source: Brett's observation, Edition 001 feedback)*

**The algorithm is the company.**
The editorial operating system — the repeatable process for transforming today's shared moments into editions that change perspective — is the true product. The software is how it scales. The algorithm is what makes it worth scaling.
*(Source: Phase 2 vision)*

## On the Framework's Relationship to the Publication

**The publication should always lead the framework. The framework should always follow.**
The framework exists to describe what the publication does, not to prescribe what each edition must do. Every structural element — the Revelation Block, the Coda slot, the Section Labels, the Reading List — should be rederived from each edition's content, not inherited from the previous edition. When a structural element appears because the previous edition had it rather than because the current edition wants it, the framework is leading the publication. This is the most dangerous failure mode of an operating system.
*(Source: Edition 002 post-edition reflection — Edition 001 became a quiet template by Edition 002)*

**Editorial taste is defined as much by what we decline as what we include.**
The hardest editorial refusals are not of weak material but of fascinating material that would make a different and possibly excellent edition — but not *this* edition. The capacity to recognize which fascinating thing is the wrong fascinating thing for a specific edition may be the irreducible core of editorial judgment.
*(Source: Edition 002 post-edition reflection — declined: racial geography of heat deaths, AC/Sun Belt history, historical panorama of heat waves)*
